# *Musical Game Design* (Music 256b / CS 476b)

## Syllabus 

**Course description**: This course expands upon and applies ideas presented in M256a/CS476a about Artful Design to the creation of artful, musical, audio-centric video games. Over 10-12 weeks of lectures, reading responses, and game-making assignments, students will learn the craft of video games (programming, rendering, music, SFX, mechanics, shaders, design process, etc.) with a focus on novel and integrated uses of audio. By the end, students will have published 3 playable games of their own design, and will be equipped to continue the critical practice of creating artful video games.

### Lectures

Below is a list of lecture ideas, tagged with #tech and/or #theory depending on their content. One way I can imagine organizing the week is by "Technique Tuesdays" and "Theory Thursdays" i.e. Tuesdays focus on practical content (code, tools, techniques) which aim to help students implement their ideas in ChucK and ChuGL. Thursdays focus on theory, aesthetics, design, etc. and help students come up with better ideas in the first place. Every creative endeavor has these two aspects, inextricably linked: (1) *what* do I want to make? and (2) *how* do I actually make it, given my time, tools, and constraints. From TAing 256a and other personal experiences, I have found each to be equally important, and also each to be a potential hangup for students, preventing them from doing their best possible work. But by speaking to both sides equally and explicitly like this, I hope that students will be empowered to both discover game ideas they never would've imagined before, and have the craft/workmanship to see those ideas through.

### Assignments

Assignments in this course will follow the critical-making, milestone-driven nature of M256a. Each assignment tasks the student with creating and publishing a videogame in ChucK/ChuGL under specific design constraints. Milestones and in-class critiques precede the final deadline. However, games need a player. The one key difference from M256a is: on the day of final deliverables, the class will experience each game by *actually playing it themselves*, ideally via a WebChuGL build on the students CCRMA page. Milestones are exempt from this requirement; the student can simply share screen and demo.

In addition, making games takes a lot of time! Respecting that, and also respecting the fact that these students will be more experienced/fluent in ChucK/ChuGL, I have assigned 3 assignments rather than 4 to encourage students to be more ambitious and go deeper with their designs.

**HW1: Create a microphone-driven minigame (1 Milestone)**. You can use keyboard and mouse, but the core gameplay mechanic should be driven by the microphone input. In my experience, this has been a great constraint for keeping the scope small but fun. You can also get away with literally not having a soundtrack or SFX, which makes me think this would be a good first-assignment.

**HW2: Video Game Genre Roulette (2 Milestones)**. Students and instructors together will create a list of common video game *genres*, (e.g. Tower Defense, Solitaire, Minesweeper, Tetris) and *modifiers* (e.g. dungeon crawler, roguelike, beat-synced, couch co-op...). Large-scale categories like "MMORPG" or "open-world" will be excluded. Students will be assigned 2-3 random genres and modifiers, and will have to create a *musical* game that combines 1 (or more!) of each. E.g. beat-synced dungeon-crawler tetris. 
- whereas HW1 has everyone creating the same kind of game, HW2 will have everyone creating something completely different. Should make for interesting design discussions! And given the limited time we have in this course, I hope this will be an effective way for students to be exposed to all sorts of different designs and genres, which can only help them with picking their final project idea. By seeing and trying more genres, they will have a better sense of what they naturally gravitate towards.

**Final Project: Make a Game (2-3 Milestones)**. Using everything you have learned this course, make a game in ChucK/ChuGL. Otherwise, no constraints.

#### Final
Like in M250a, finals day for this class could be a public showcase/arcade of student projects. Students set up their games in either the stage or classroom. Begin by having each student give a quick 1-2 minute intro on their game, and then transition into a freeplay where people can roam around and try each others' games.

### Video Game Show-And-Tell

In Hassan's Studio Recording, every class began with a student sharing a personal favorite recording. The class first would listen together, and then talk about it briefly before transitioning into the lecture of the day. Students signed up for a timeslot in the first week of class. Depending on the size of the class, we could do something similar--students (and instructors) each are assigned one day of the quarter to begin class with an brief, informal (but passionate!) introduction to a personally-important video game.

### AI Policy

There will be no AI usage in this class whatsoever. Not for programming, not for summarizing readings, not for writing your reading responses, not even for ideation. If you are stuck on a programming or game design problem, come to OH or reach out to your peers on the class Discord. If you're running out of time to finish a reading response, just don't do it, it's okay.
- I hope it comes as a relief to students that they will be given the liberty to learn at their own pace, on their own terms, without having to compete with others who do use AI in a Prisoner's-Dilemma-type nightmare situation.
- I want to foster the instinct of critical thinking and reaching out to friends/peers/community/humans when things start getting hard, not AI. As a class we ought to be a community that trusts and encourages sharing our successes *and* struggles with each other. Also, some of my best design ideas have come from being stuck, from *not knowing*, and being forced to find an alternative path forwards.
- This AI policy isn't a tradeoff; it's a win-win. Instructors will be valued, and students will learn more and produce better, more meaningful work.
