### Video Game Show-And-Tell

In Hassan's Studio Recording, every class began with a student sharing a personal favorite recording. The class first would listen together, and then talk about it briefly before transitioning into the lecture of the day. Students signed up for a timeslot in the first week of class. Depending on the size of the class, we could do something similar--students (and instructors) each are assigned one day of the quarter to begin class with an brief, informal (but passionate!) introduction to a personally-important video game.

### Lectures

Below is a list of lecture ideas, tagged with #tech and/or #theory depending on their content. One way I can imagine organizing the week is by "Technique Tuesdays" and "Theory Thursdays" i.e. Tuesdays focus on practical content (code, tools, techniques) which aim to help students implement their ideas in ChucK and ChuGL. Thursdays focus on theory, aesthetics, design, etc. and help students come up with better ideas in the first place. Every creative endeavor has these two aspects, inextricably linked: (1) *what* do I want to make? and (2) *how* do I actually make it, given my time, tools, and constraints. From TAing 256a and other personal experiences, I have found each to be equally important, and also each to be a potential hangup for students, preventing them from doing their best possible work. In the medium of video games, in particular, implementation  Of course you cannot fully divorce technique and theory from each other, and that is not the intention. But by speaking to both sides equally and explicitly like this, I hope that students will be empowered to both discover game ideas they never would've imagined before, and have the craft/workmanship to see those ideas through.
